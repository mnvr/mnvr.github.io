Shiva

“Repetition is the shadow of structure”, I read somewhere. This is true in code, philosophy, life. That escalated fast, as is common in most lines of thought I follow along for a while; all roads lead to Rome indeed, and not only that, when I get down low enough the dependencies become circular.

It is true, but incomplete, for each repetition is different. This is where I used to, and still trip up, say when trying to find useful abstractions in the programs I write. Consistency is the hobgoblin of small minds, but most of my mind is small, and it helps me remain sane; still sometimes I am necessitated to step outside and face the sharp edges of reification to find the structure that generates the repetition that I'm trying to replicate.

In the beginning, Brahma created the universe, but it was lifeless, repetitive.

Not knowing what to do, he went to Shiva, blankly pointing at his creation. It was Shiva who made it alive.

What Shiva does, is, goes by many names. Destruction. Chaos. Randomness. It is that makes repetitions alive.

God does not play dice, Einstein, he is dice.

Entropy

Consider 4 lights. Each pattern they form is a microstate.

Entropy is the count of microstates similar to the one whose entropy we seek. This is low entropy, since there are only 2 like this (all on, or all off).

This is high entropy, since there are 8 like this (only 1 on or 1 off).

The log converts the count into the number of questions we need to ask the universe to determine which exact microstate a system is in, when we know its macrostate.

Viewed this way, the second law is a statement of mathematics.

If a light flips, the 2 low entropy microstates are more likely to transition into one of the other higher entropy ones than vice versa.

Equity of transitions is where physics comes in.

“Chance is a more fundamental conception that causality”
— Max Born

Simplified variant of an explanation in Antonio Padilla's Fantastic Numbers. Manav, Feb 2025.

Mind

The first memories from childhood, the last dream I had. I have been there in all of them.

My favorite books have changed, so has the way I look, or the way I like my coffee. I know there is a brain that underlies this activity, and there are these thoughts that float above it, and both of these are constantly changing. When I look close, I can see my mind as distinct from both of these.

Something hasn't changed, or so it seems. There has been something that has been always there, witnessing.

Is that me? Or is that my mind? Am I my mind?

The mind is a series of changes, the ocean on which the waves wave, but the consciousness does not change. The thoughts are the waves, that much is clear, but is the mind the ocean, or consciounsess; are they different, or are they the same?

And then there is that other question. Is my mind in this world,
or is the world in my mind?
